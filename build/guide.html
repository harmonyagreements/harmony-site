Guide to the Contributor Agreements

The Template Agreements commence with an introduction to ensure that the contributor understands that this agreement is legally binding. Section 1 provides the defined terms used throughout the Template Agreements. Section 2 provides for the "Inbound" transfer (either license or assignment) by which the contributor makes his contribution available to the Project Entity. This section also includes the five options for licenses under which the Project Entity may license the contribution on an "outbound" license. For example, the inbound licenses are generally very broad to enable the Template Agreement to cover any of the FOSS Licenses as an "outbound" license, from Apache Software License to GPLv2. This section also includes a separate, broader license for media (materials other than software). Section 3 includes promises confirming that the contributor has both the necessary rights to make the transfer and that the transfer does not conflict with other rights that have been given to third parties. This section also includes a section which deals with "mixed contributions" which means contributions including works whose rights are owned by third parties. Finally, the Template Agreement includes two sections dealing with "disclaimers" of certain legal provisions under Article 2 (see discussion of on "Other Laws"). A final section deals with a variety of legal issues which are common to contracts, such as choice of governing law and waivers of certain rights.

Introduction

The introduction identifies the contributor and the Project Entity and ensures that the contributor understands that the individual or company is entering into a binding legal agreement. The assumption of all of the Template Agreements is that the FOSS project is managed by an entity, whether it is a foundation, non profit corporation or for profit corporation. This section also provides the opportunity for the Project Entity to establish the means by which the Project Entity will make the agreement legally effective. Project Entities may take different approaches to making the agreement effective; the most traditional, and most legally conservative, approach is to have signed agreements. However, Project Entities may decide that they would prefer to employ digital signatures or conditional licenses and that choice is left open to the particular Project Entity. This section makes clear that the agreement may apply to more than one software project managed by a Project Entity.

Definitions

The definitions section is a critical part of the contributor agreement. The use of definitions enables the contributor agreement to avoid repeating various descriptions in the rest of the agreement.

The definition of "Contribution" is the most important definition because it describes the nature of what is actually being either licensed or assigned to the Project Entity. This provision is based on definitions of "Contribution" in FOSS Licenses. The definition is also based on ownership of the copyright in the work of authorship which can be text, media or code that is "Submitted" (an equally important definition). We have also added a new definition, "Submission"(which includes all works which are "submitted"), to reflect the fact that many contributors submit works which include works of third parties. "Submitted" means contributions provided by any form of electronic, verbal or written communication adopted by the Project Entity; however, a contributor may mark a submission to indicate that it is not a Contribution. For example, contributors may submit patches in the form of the entire software program and such submissions include the base software program whose copyright is probably not owned by the contributor of the patches. After considerable discussion, we decided to limit contribution to works of authorship in which the contributor owns the copyright in order to enable Project Entities to determine how to deal with "mixed" submissions. Project Entities may take a variety of measures to determine whether or not third party code may be provided to the Project Entity: from a certificate of origin to a more elaborate procedure (see the procedure adopted by the Eclipse Foundation). We have provided that the Project Entity can determine the procedure for dealing with "mixed" submissions and provide a link to it in Section 3(d).

"Copyright" includes a general definition of copyright law as well as "neighboring rights." As noted above, copyright varies by country. Thus, an individual or a company does not own a "copyright," but rather owns rights arising under the (i) copyright law of the United States (in the United States), (ii) copyright law of France (in France) and (iii) copyright law of Germany (in Germany). Neighboring rights are those similar to copyrights, such as database rights, public lending rights, moral rights and performers rights. Most of these rights do not apply to software, but may apply to other works of authorship such as text, video and images. Such rights are included in the Template Agreements because they apply to both software and related works, such as documentation, images and video.

"Effective Date" is defined to ensure that Contributions are covered even if the Contributor Agreement is executed after the date of submission of the Contribution.

"Legal Entity" is based on the definition of "Legal Entity" found in FOSS Licenses and includes the various forms of corporate entities; through Affiliates it includes other members of the corporate family. The definition of Affiliates includes parent companies, sister companies and subsidiary companies. As noted in the definition of "You," these definitions are needed to deal with the complexities of the ownership of intellectual property rights within companies.

"Patent Claims" is based on the definition of patent claims found in FOSS Licenses which provide patent licenses to users of the Contribution. We have included the definition in the alternative to cover the various manners in which patent claims are used in FOSS Licenses. For corporations, we have defined Patent Claims to include patents owned by the corporate entity submitting the Contribution as well as other members of its corporate family (its Affiliates).

"Media" is all of the works of authorship which are not code. The most common types of works of authorship in this category are documentation, images and videos. We have separated out these types of works because FOSS Licenses are designed to license solely software. In a significant number of cases, they do not include licenses of sufficient breadth to permit a Project Entity to use these works in the manner which is common, such as including the documentation on wikis. Thus, the Template Agreements give much broader rights for "outbound" licenses for media than they do for "outbound" licenses for software.

"Work" is the software project for which a Contribution has been Submitted.

"Submission" is defined to include the work of authorship which is submitted and which may include works whose copyright is owned by third parties. For example, contributors may submit patches in the form of the entire software program and such submissions include the base software program whose copyright is probably not owned by the contributor of the patches. After considerable discussion, we decided to limit Contribution to works of authorship in which the Contributor owns the copyright in order to enable Project Entities to determine how to deal with "mixed" submissions.

"Submitted" is based on definitions in FOSS Licenses and defines the procedure by which a contributor communicates with a Project Entity in providing a Contribution. It also provides that a contributor may submit a work but make clear that it is not being "Submitted" as a Contribution.

"Work" is the all of the software projects which is being distributed by the Project Entity to third parties, but does not include the Contribution.

"You" describes in more detail who is making the Contribution. This definition is used primarily to deal with corporations to ensure that the contributor includes the corporate entity which owns or has the rights to appropriate intellectual property rights in the Contribution. Many corporations have split up the ownership of their intellectual property in complicated ways to minimize taxes. Thus, the corporate entity which employs the individual who creates the Contribution may not own the intellectual property rights in the Contribution. For example, some large companies have all their intellectual property owned by a Swiss subsidiary even though the companies may otherwise do very little business in Switzerland.

Transfer Options

The Template Agreements are divided into two forms: license and assignment.

Copyright License Version

Section 2.1(a) merely clarifies that the license transfer option enables the contributor to retain ownership of both the copyright and the patents which are the subjects of the license. Section 2.1(b) includes a very broad license under the relevant copyrights which includes all of the traditional copyright rights such as reproduction, distribution, modification, display and performance (we have not included "public performance" and "public display" because of potential differences in copyright laws in other countries). The license is perpetual and irrevocable. In addition, the license permits the Project Entity to sublicense these rights through multiple tiers of sublicensees: the license permits the project to sublicense to its users and those users to in turn sublicense to their users. Although some open source licenses provide a "single" tier license such as GPLv2 and do not permit sublicensing, many other FOSS Licenses expressly permit sublicensing. However, the license grant is "conditioned" on the requirement that the Project Entity may only provide the work under a limited number of "outbound" licenses (see below).

Section 2.1(c) provides a patent license that grants a license of sufficient scope to be consistent with the scope of the patent license in the "outbound" license in the FOSS License. For example, the patent license in Apache Software License 2.0 is different from the patent license in the Mozilla Public License.

Section 2.1(d) in the Template Agreement provides that the "outbound licenses" under which the Project Entity may license the Contribution. The Project Entity must choose among seven mutually exclusive options on "outbound license", all of which include the license or licenses being used by the Project Entity on the Submission Date for the Work ("Original Licenses") (this provision also includes the right to use future versions of such licenses, such as a move from GPLv2 to GPLv3 if the Project Entity chooses to keep that option). The options are:

  • Option One: The Original Licenses
  • Option Two: The Original Licenses and additional licenses which are listed.
  • Option Three: The Original Licenses and additional licenses which have been approved by the Open Source Initiative on the Effective Date (whether or not the license is later disapproved, such as the Common Public License).
  • Option Four: The Original Licenses and additional licenses which have been recommended by the Free Software Foundation on the Effective Date (whether or not the license is later no longer recommended).
  • Option Five: Licenses of any type, whether FOSS Licenses or proprietary licenses, but with the requirement that the contribution must always be licensed under the Original Licenses.

Copyright Assignment Version

Section 2.1(a) provides for a worldwide assignment of copyrights. In some jurisdictions, copyrights may not be assigned. Sections 2.1(b) and 2.1(c) provides for alternative methods of ensuring that in those jurisdictions to achieve the same result. However, the assignment is "conditioned" on the requirement that the Project Entity may only provide the work on a limited number of "outbound" licenses (see below).

At the same time as the assignment, Section 2.1(d) provides for an immediate very broad license back of the assigned copyrights to the contributor to enable the contributor to use the contribution for any purpose. The license back is of the same scope as copyright license in the license transfer option.

Section 2.1(e) provides a patent license of the same scope as in the other option. The patent license grants a license of sufficient scope to be consistent with the scope of the patent licenses in the "outbound" license in the FOSS License. For example, the patent license in Apache Software License 2.0 is different from the patent license in the Mozilla Public License.

Section 2.1(f) in the Template Agreement provides that the "outbound licenses" under which the Project Entity may license the Contribution. The Project Entity must choose among seven mutually exclusive options on "outbound license", all of which include the license or licenses being used by the Project Entity on the Submission Date for the Work ("Original Licenses") (this provision also includes the right to use future versions of such licenses, such as a move from GPLv2 to GPLv3 if the Project Entity chooses to keep that option). The options are:

  • Option One: The Original Licenses
  • Option Two: The Original Licenses and additional licenses which are listed.
  • Option Three: The Original Licenses and additional licenses which have been approved by the Open Source Initiative on the Effective Date (whether or not the license is later disapproved, such as the Common Public License).
  • Option Four: The Original Licenses and additional licenses which have been recommended by the Free Software Foundation on the Effective Date (whether or not the license is later no longer recommended).
  • Option Five: Licenses of any type, whether FOSS Licenses or proprietary licenses, but with the requirement that the contribution must always be licensed under the Original Licenses. a license selected by the Project Entity from the Original License or (ii) at least one license selected by the Project Entity which (i) has been approved by the Open Source Initiative on the Effective Date (whether or not the license is later disapproved) or (ii) has been recommended by the Free Software Foundation on the Effective Date (whether or not the license is later no longer recommended).

Section 2.2 provides that Media may be licensed to third parties in "outbound licenses" to additional listed licenses permitted under Section 2.1. As we noted, in many cases documentation may need to be licensed under broader terms than are permitted by FOSS Licenses, and thus we have the option to include additional outbound licenses for Media only (i.e. any work that is not software). We expect that in many cases these additional licenses will be Creative Commons licenses which are more appropriate for such works.

Section 2.3 waives moral rights. Moral rights are those rights which permit an author of a copyrightable work to control the modification of the work or his name associated with the work. Many countries have limited or eliminated these rights in the case of software, but the Template Agreement also includes licenses to Media which may be subject to moral rights.

Section 2.4 makes clear that the Project Entity is not required to use a contribution simply because it has been submitted.

Section 2.5 deals with certain cases which provide that licenses may be "implied" unless this type of provision is included.

Agreement

Section 3 provides standard legal promises to ensure that the contributor has the appropriate rights to provide the transfers in Section 2. These promises are in three parts:

  • Section 3(a) confirms that the contributor has legal authority to enter into the Template Agreement. For individuals, this promise is a reminder to employees who may need approval of their employers and minors who may need approval of guardians or parents. In addition, corporations generally have a limited number of individuals who can bind the corporation.
  • Section 3(b) confirms that the individual or corporation has the rights needed to provide the transfers. This issue is particularly important for corporations that may have the intellectual property rights divided among different entities for tax purposes.
  • Section 3(c) provides that the transfer obligations in Section 2 do not violate any other agreements. This issue is particularly important for employees of corporations who, if they have developed the contribution during their employment, may not have the rights to grant the transfers in Section 2.

Section 3.4(d) provides the Project Entity with an opportunity to deal with the issues of software whose copyright is owned by third parties in a Submission. As noted above and after considerable discussion, we decided to limit Contribution to works of authorship in which the contributor owns the copyright in order to enable Project Entities to determine how to deal with "mixed" submissions. Project Entities may take a variety of measures to determine whether or not third party code may be provided to the Project Entity: from a certificate of origin to a more elaborate procedure (see the procedure adopted by the Eclipse Foundation).

Disclaimer of Certain Implied Warranties

Section 4 disclaims the "implied" provisions found in Section 2 and certain other consumer protection statutes which impose obligations. As noted, local laws such as Article 2 may imply certain warranties into all software licenses unless properly disclaimed. Article 2 requires that the disclaimer of the warranties of merchantability and fitness for this particular purpose must be "conspicuous." This requirement requires that the disclaimers must be in bold, capitalized or in different colors. Given the variety of text forms that are used in software licenses, we have included these disclaimers in capital letters. The requirements for disclaiming the warranty of non infringement are not clear, but a disclaimer in capital letters will disclaim this particular warranty. In the assignment Template Agreement, the disclaimer is also from the Project Entity to the contributor because of the license back to the contribution in Section 2.1(d).

Waiver of Certain Types of Damages

Section 5 eliminates the potential liability for consequential damages (lost profits) which would otherwise be available under Article 2. Section 2-719(3) permits parties to limit or exclude consequential damages unless doing so would be unconscionable. In the assignment form of the Template Agreement, the waiver is also from the Project Entity to the contributor because of the license back to the contribution in Section 2.1(d).

Miscellaneous

Section 6 governs a number of issues which are standard for all contracts. Section 6.1 provides for the choice of law. All contracts are governed by a particular law and the same words may be interpreted differently in different jurisdictions. If a contract does not include a choice of governing law, the first issue in any dispute will be determining the applicable law. The selection of the governing law is determined by a body of law known as conflict of laws. This area of law can produce different outcomes for the same set of facts if the contributor and Project Entity are in different jurisdictions. After significant discussion, we decided that it was better for FOSS projects to have predictability in interpretation of the terms of the contributor agreement. In addition, the term disclaims a treaty, the United Nations Convention on the International Sale of Goods ("UNCISG"), which is similar to the UCC and would apply automatically to the contributor agreement if the parties are in different countries which are each members of the UNCISG. UNCISG has rarely been used and is an unpredictable combination of civil law and common law, and thus would lead to further ambiguity about the interpretation of the terms of the contributor agreement.

Section 6.2 deals with other standard issues. The first sentence is an "integration" provision which ensures that the terms of the agreement are only those stated in the contributor agreement and excludes all other discussions or other agreements prior to the contributor agreement. The second sentence permits the agreement to be assigned either by the Project or by the contributor but any assignment is conditioned on the assignee agreeing to the terms of the contributor agreement. This provision was the subject to significant discussion, but we believe that this flexibility is very important for Projects. Such permission is required for non exclusive copyright licenses under United States law. Since Projects may change entities or otherwise need this flexibility in the future, we have protected the expectation of the contributors by requiring that the assignee agree to the terms. The third sentence ensures that a single "waiver" of the rights of a party does not imply a future violation of the same provision. For example, if a Project Entity had promised to only use the GPLv2 as an "outbound license," the Project Entity uses the Apache Software License and a contributor does not complain about this violation over a period of time, the contributor might be deemed to have waived that change. This provision ensures that such a waiver would not occur. The fourth sentence provides that if a court finds a provision of the agreement to be unenforceable under the relevant laws, the court will replace the unenforceable provision with a provision that comes the closest to the intentions of the original provision. The final sentence deals with Article 2 provisions that have occasionally been used to not enforce a consequential damages waiver under Sections 2-719(2) and (3). We are trying to ensure that it is as clear as possible that it is the intention of the parties to prohibit the application of this doctrine to set aside the limitations on remedies, including the waiver of consequential damages.
